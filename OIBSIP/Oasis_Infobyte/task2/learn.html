Tribute page on Albert Einstein

Biography

Albert Einstein was a German-born theoretical physicist who developed the theory of relativity, one of the two pillars of modern physics (alongside quantum mechanics). He is best known to the general public for his mass-energy equivalence formula E = mc², which has been dubbed "the world's most famous equation". He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 "for his services to Theoretical Physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect".

In one of Albert Einstein’s revolutionary scientific papers published in 1905, E=mc 2 was introduced; where E is energy, m is mass, and c is the speed of light in a vacuum. Since then, E=mc 2 has become one of the most famous equations in the world.

Early Life

Einstein was born in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg in the German Empire, on 14 March 1879 into a family of secular Ashkenazi Jews. His father, Hermann Einstein, was a salesman and engineer who, with his brother, founded Elektrotechnische Fabrik J. Einstein & Cie (a company that manufactured electrical equipment based on direct current).

Einstein attended a Catholic elementary school in Munich, where he excelled in his studies. At age 10, he began attending the Luitpold Gymnasium (now called the Albert Einstein Gymnasium), though he did not enjoy the school's style of rote learning. He left Germany for Italy at the age of 15 to finish secondary school, but he dropped out at the age of 16 and went to Switzerland, where he enrolled in a four-year mathematics and physics teaching diploma program at the Eidgenössische Polytechnische Schule in Zürich (later the ETH Zürich).

Career

After graduation, Einstein worked as a patent clerk in Bern, Switzerland, which gave him time to pursue his own scientific interests. In 1905, he published a series of groundbreaking papers, including one on the photoelectric effect, which helped establish the particle theory of light. He also published papers on Brownian motion and the special theory of relativity, which he developed to reconcile the laws of mechanics with the laws of electromagnetism.

In 1915, Einstein completed the general theory of relativity, which extends the special theory to cover accelerated frames of reference and gravity. This theory is the basis for modern cosmology and our understanding of the structure of the universe.
